# Flywheel Affiliate Daily

## The Lead

**Experian's "Agent Trust" Gives AI Shopping Agents an Identity — And That Changes Everything for Affiliates**

Agentic commerce just got its infrastructure moment. On Wednesday, Experian announced Agent Trust, a first-of-its-kind framework that creates a verified link between human consumers and the AI agents acting on their behalf. Visa, Cloudflare, and Skyfire are collaborators. The system works by issuing real-time trust tokens that bind a verified human identity to an AI agent and its transaction intent — what Experian calls "Know Your Agent," a deliberate echo of Know Your Customer protocols in financial services.

This matters for affiliate marketing far more than it appears on the surface. The fundamental blocker for AI-agent-as-affiliate has been trust: when a bot shows up to buy something on behalf of a human, the merchant has no way to verify that the transaction is legitimate, authorized, or even wanted. That ambiguity has kept brands cautious about opening their affiliate programs to AI traffic. Agent Trust solves the identity problem at the protocol level — and when identity is solved, attribution follows.

Consider what happens next. If an AI agent carries a verified trust token linking it to a specific consumer, networks can attribute that agent's product recommendations and purchases the same way they attribute a human publisher's clicks. The agent becomes a trackable, commissionable entity in the affiliate ecosystem. Experian's Chief Innovation Officer Kathleen Peters put it directly: "Agentic commerce will not scale without trust." She's right — and now the trust layer exists.

The coalition behind this is as telling as the product itself. Visa brings its Intelligent Commerce and Trusted Agent Protocol. Cloudflare enforces trust at the network edge, where it already handles roughly 20% of global internet traffic. Skyfire provides an open, standardized way to package agent-related information across platforms. This isn't a startup demo — it's a stack built by companies that already underpin global commerce. Vendors should treat this as a signal that agentic commerce infrastructure is moving from whitepaper to production.

## What's Moving

- **Wayward launches Boost, a platform scaling partnership media into a performance channel.** The patent-pending product treats partner content placements as measurable, attributable performance media — blurring the line between branded content and affiliate. Why it matters: partnership media has been "effective but constrained" by scale limitations; Boost attempts to solve that with programmatic-style distribution while keeping the trust signal of partner endorsements.

- **Meta's Facebook Affiliate Partnerships program expands beyond Amazon.** eBay has joined the beta, and Shopee is live across eight markets in Southeast Asia and Latin America. Creators can now tag products directly in Facebook posts and Reels without link-in-bio workarounds. Why it matters: Meta is building a native affiliate layer inside its social graph — the biggest distribution surface on earth is becoming a shoppable affiliate channel.

- **US affiliate marketing spend hits $13.81 billion in 2026, per eMarketer.** The headline number is less interesting than the subtext: measurement frameworks still can't capture affiliate's full value, AI chatbots are reshaping discovery, and creators are claiming a growing share of budgets. Why it matters: the channel is growing into a measurement gap — which means vendors who invest in better attribution now will outperform those waiting for the industry to catch up.

- **MarTech argues affiliate marketing "still needs humans in the AI era."** The piece makes the case that while AI handles optimization and content generation at scale, human expertise in relationship management, strategic partner selection, and creative judgment remains irreplaceable. Why it matters: the winning strategy isn't AI vs. humans — it's using AI to amplify what human affiliate managers already do well.

- **Global affiliate spend reaches $19.4 billion, nearly doubling from $9.6 billion in 2020.** Performance-based influencer compensation has crossed 53% adoption among creator programs. Why it matters: the structural shift from flat-fee creator deals to performance-based models is now the majority position — vendors still running fixed-rate influencer programs are paying above market.

## Partner Landscape

**Profile: Loyalty and Cashback Platforms**

Loyalty and cashback platforms occupy a unique position in the affiliate ecosystem — they're the only partner type where the consumer's primary motivation is the reward, not the content. Rakuten Rewards (20M+ US members), Ibotta, TopCashback, and Capital One Shopping collectively drive billions in attributable commerce by offering consumers money back on purchases they were already considering.

In 2026, this partner category is undergoing two shifts. First, the Rakuten–impact.com alliance means the largest cashback platform in the US will soon run on impact.com's infrastructure, potentially changing how loyalty publishers interact with the network. Merchants currently on Rakuten should expect migration conversations by summer. Second, AI agents are beginning to replicate the core loyalty value proposition — Wildfire Systems has launched the first AI-specific loyalty platform, enabling cashback and rewards on AI-assisted purchases. If your AI shopping agent can earn you 5% back on a recommendation, the line between a loyalty platform and an AI agent functionally disappears.

For vendors, the actionable insight is this: cashback partners convert at high rates because they capture purchase-intent traffic. But commission economics are tight — loyalty publishers negotiate aggressively on rates, and the consumer sees the brand as interchangeable. The antidote is exclusive offers: give cashback platforms a deal their members can't get elsewhere, and you earn brand preference inside a channel that otherwise treats you as a commodity.

## The AI Angle

Experian's Agent Trust launch this week is the clearest signal yet that agentic commerce is moving from concept to infrastructure. With Visa, Cloudflare, and Skyfire building verified identity binding between humans and AI agents, the last major trust barrier to AI-agent-driven affiliate commerce is being addressed at the protocol level. Meanwhile, Amazon CEO Andy Jassy told investors this week that Rufus, Amazon's AI shopping assistant, has "improved substantially" and called the agentic commerce opportunity "very good for customers." When both identity infrastructure companies and the world's largest retailer are investing in the same direction, the timeline for AI agents becoming a material affiliate channel just accelerated.
