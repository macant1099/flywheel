# Flywheel Affiliate Daily

## The Lead

**Rakuten Hands Its Affiliate Platform to Impact.com — And Reshapes the Entire Network Map**

The affiliate industry's two longest-running rivals just became partners. On Monday, Rakuten International and impact.com announced a strategic alliance in which Rakuten will stop operating its own affiliate technology platform entirely. Thousands of merchants and publishers currently on Rakuten's dashboard will be migrated to impact.com's infrastructure, which becomes the exclusive platform provider for Rakuten's commerce and partnership management business. No equity changed hands. No acquisition was announced. This is a pure strategic merger of capabilities — and it rewires the competitive map of the entire affiliate industry.

The deal is significant because it removes a standalone network from the market and concentrates platform power. For over two decades, advertisers chose between Rakuten, Impact, CJ, Awin, and ShareASale as the primary affiliate network platforms. That list just got shorter. Impact CEO David Yovanno framed the move as the two companies "aligning on a unified value prop" to compete with Google, Amazon, and Meta on scale and always-on conversion budgets. Rakuten International CEO Amit Patel pushed back on the characterization that Rakuten is "getting out of the technology business," saying the company will continue investing in AI, measurement, and automation — but within its owned assets like Rakuten Rewards (20M+ US members) and its agency services, not in the SaaS platform layer.

What does this mean for vendors? Three things. First, if you run affiliate programs on Rakuten Advertising, a migration to impact.com is coming — likely by mid-summer. Your tracking pixels, API integrations, and publisher relationships need to be documented now so the transition doesn't break attribution. Second, this consolidation gives impact.com enormous leverage as a platform. Combined with Rakuten's publisher base, Impact now manages one of the largest affiliate ecosystems on earth. Expect pricing and terms conversations to shift. Third, Rakuten Rewards becomes a pure publisher — the biggest loyalty and cashback publisher in the US, now operating exclusively on someone else's rails. That changes the negotiation dynamics for every merchant that treats Rakuten as both platform and partner.

The deeper signal is about what the industry thinks it needs to survive. Yovanno admitted affiliate marketing has been "that awkward kid" in digital advertising for years. The alliance is a bet that consolidation — not fragmentation — is how the channel earns its seat at the table against walled gardens. Whether that bet pays off depends on execution, but the direction is clear: the affiliate industry is entering its consolidation era.

## What's Moving

- **Banks are missing the affiliate opportunity, per a new Fintel Connect/Cornerstone Advisors report.** 43% of financial institution marketers don't incorporate affiliate into planning until after budgets are set, and many fold it into broader performance buckets with no distinct measurement. Why it matters: banking and fintech represent one of affiliate's highest-commission verticals (often $50-200+ per lead), but institutional budgeting practices are leaving money on the table.

- **Performance-based creator contracts now the majority compensation model.** 53% of creator programs use performance-based (affiliate-style) compensation, up from under 30% two years ago. Brands are shifting to multi-month "ambassador" deals where repeated exposure through a trusted creator drives better recall and lifetime value. Why it matters: if your influencer program still runs on flat fees, you're paying above market and getting worse attribution.

- **A2A (Agent-to-Agent) protocol reaches 150+ production deployments.** The Linux Foundation's April 2026 release confirms the protocol — which lets AI agents communicate and transact with each other — is live across Microsoft Azure AI Foundry, Amazon Bedrock AgentCore, and Salesforce. Why it matters: A2A is the plumbing that lets shopping agents negotiate with merchant agents autonomously, and it just crossed from pilot to production scale.

- **eMarketer publishes definitive 2026 affiliate FAQ: AI and creators are the two forces reshaping the channel.** Key finding: measurement frameworks still can't capture affiliate's full value, creating a gap that favors brands investing in better attribution. Why it matters: the industry's biggest problem isn't growth — it's proving the growth it's already generating.

- **Visa formally launches Intelligent Commerce, an initiative for AI agents to transact on behalf of consumers.** Combined with Experian's Agent Trust (launched last week), the payments layer for agentic commerce is now live from two of the world's largest financial infrastructure companies. Why it matters: when Visa and Experian both build agent-commerce products in the same week, the question shifts from "will AI agents buy things?" to "how fast?"

## Partner Landscape

**Profile: AI Shopping Agents as Affiliate Partners**

The newest entrant in the affiliate partner taxonomy doesn't have a website, a social following, or a content calendar. It's an AI agent — and it's increasingly behaving like a publisher.

AI shopping agents like Amazon's Rufus, Perplexity's shopping features, and a growing ecosystem of independent agents built on frameworks like OpenAI's and Google's are now recommending products, comparing prices, and in some cases completing purchases autonomously. The affiliate question is straightforward: when an AI agent drives a sale, who earns the commission?

Today, most AI-agent-driven commerce bypasses affiliate tracking entirely. The agent queries product APIs, synthesizes information, and presents a recommendation — but there's no click, no cookie, and no affiliate link in the traditional sense. This is why infrastructure like Experian's Agent Trust and Visa's Intelligent Commerce matters: they create the identity and attribution layers that could make AI agents trackable and commissionable partners.

The early movers are already here. Wildfire Systems has launched an AI-specific loyalty platform. Microsoft's Copilot integrates shopping recommendations with affiliate attribution built in. And the A2A protocol now in production at 150+ organizations provides the communication standard for agent-to-merchant negotiation.

For vendors, the practical advice is to start thinking about AI agents the way you think about any high-volume publisher: what data do they need to recommend your products accurately? Are your product feeds optimized for machine consumption, not just human browsing? Do your affiliate terms allow automated traffic? The brands that answer these questions first will capture the earliest wave of agentic commerce revenue — a wave that Mastercard says already converts 15-20% higher than traditional search.

## The AI Angle

This was the week agentic commerce went from theoretical to infrastructural. Experian's Agent Trust, Visa's Intelligent Commerce, and the A2A protocol crossing 150 production deployments all landed within days of each other. Add Rakuten's exit from the platform layer — which frees it to focus on becoming the premier publisher for AI-agent-driven loyalty — and the outlines of a new affiliate architecture are visible: identity verification (Experian), payment rails (Visa), agent communication (A2A), and distribution (Rakuten Rewards + impact.com). The stack is assembling in real time.

## One Thing to Do Today

**Audit your product data feed for machine readability.** Pull up your primary affiliate feed and ask: could an AI agent parse this to make an accurate product recommendation? Check for complete, structured attributes — price, availability, shipping, return policy, product category, and unique identifiers (GTINs/UPCs). AI agents don't browse product pages; they consume structured data. Ten minutes reviewing your feed now positions you for the channel that Visa, Experian, and every major tech company just bet their infrastructure on.
